Module 1 — Lesson 1

Why Most People Get Bad Outputs — And the One Shift That Fixes Everything

The AI Prompting Course

There's a moment I think about a lot. I was sitting at my desk, staring at a response from Claude that was technically correct but completely useless to me. I'd asked it to help me write an email to an investor, and what I got back read like a Wikipedia article crossed with a cover letter from 2007. Stiff. Over-formal. Full of phrases no human being would ever actually say out loud.

And my first instinct — honestly — was to think, "This tool isn't that good."

I see this all the time now. People try AI once, get something mediocre, and walk away thinking they've seen what it can do. They haven't. What they've seen is what happens when you talk to something incredibly capable without giving it any real information to work with.

That's what this lesson is about. Not prompting tricks or hacks. Something more fundamental — a shift in how you think about the conversation you're having when you type something into an AI tool.

The Real Problem

Here's the pattern I see over and over. Someone opens up Claude or ChatGPT and types something like:

"Write me an email to a client."

And then they get back something generic. A perfectly structured, grammatically correct email that could've been written by anyone, about anything, to anyone. And they're disappointed.

But think about it for a second. If you walked up to a coworker — someone brilliant, someone you trust — and said "Write me an email to a client," they'd look at you and say, "Okay... which client? About what? What's the situation? What tone? What are you trying to get out of this?"

They wouldn't just start writing. They'd need context.

AI is the same way. The difference is that AI won't push back. It won't ask you those questions. It'll just do its best with what you gave it, which was almost nothing. And "doing its best with almost nothing" means defaulting to the safest, most generic version of whatever you asked for.

That's the gap. The gap between what you wanted and what you got isn't a technology problem. It's an information problem. You knew what you meant. The AI didn't.

The Shift

The one shift that changes everything is this: stop thinking of AI as a search engine and start thinking of it as a collaborator who just walked into the room.

A search engine needs a query. A collaborator needs context.

When I work with Claude now — and I use it constantly, for everything from investor emails to ad copy to analyzing market data — I don't start with what I want it to produce. I start with what it needs to know.

That's it. That's the entire shift. Before you type what you want, give it what it needs.

Let me show you what I mean.

The Example

Let's say I need to write a follow-up email to someone I met at a conference. They run a mid-size SaaS company, we had a quick conversation about a potential partnership, and I want to reconnect without being pushy.

Here's what most people type:

"Write a follow-up email after meeting someone at a conference."

And here's what they get — something like:

"Dear [Name], It was a pleasure meeting you at [Conference Name]. I enjoyed our conversation about [Topic]. I would love to explore potential synergies between our organizations. Please let me know if you would be available for a call at your earliest convenience."

It's fine. It's also forgettable, sounds like it was written by a robot, and could've been sent by literally anyone on earth. It's the email equivalent of elevator music.

Now here's how I'd actually prompt it:

"I met James Park at SaaStr Annual last week. He's the CEO of a mid-size SaaS company doing around $15M ARR in the HR tech space. We talked for about ten minutes at the after-party — casual conversation, not a formal meeting. He mentioned they're struggling with their outbound sales process and I think there might be a real partnership opportunity with what we're building. I want to follow up in a way that feels warm and natural, not salesy. Keep it short — five or six sentences max. I write pretty casually in emails, no corporate speak."

Now Claude has something to work with. It knows who James is, what his company does, how we met, the vibe of our conversation, what I'm hoping to accomplish, and how I actually communicate. The output is going to be completely different — specific, natural, and actually useful.

That's the shift in action. Same task. Wildly different results. The only thing that changed was the amount of context I gave it.

Why This Works

There's something going on under the hood that's worth understanding, even if you never want to get technical about it.

AI language models are, at their core, prediction machines. They're predicting the most likely next word based on everything that came before it. When you give a vague prompt, the prediction has to be safe. It has to work for the broadest possible interpretation of what you might have meant. So it defaults to the average — the most generic, least risky version of whatever you asked for.

When you load up the prompt with real, specific context, you narrow the prediction space. You're essentially telling the model, "You're not writing for everyone. You're writing for this specific situation, this specific person, in this specific tone." And the output snaps into focus.

Think of it like giving directions. "Go somewhere fun" could mean a thousand things. "Take me to that Thai place on 5th Street, the one with the outdoor patio" gets you exactly where you want to go. Same idea.

The Concept: Context Is the Input That Matters Most

I want you to walk away from this lesson with one belief locked in: the quality of your output is directly proportional to the quality of your input.

Not the cleverness of your prompt. Not the specific words you use. Not whether you said "please" or "act as an expert." The quality of the context. The real, specific, situational information that helps the AI understand exactly what you're dealing with.

Here's why this matters so much. Most of the prompting advice you'll find online is about tricks. "Use this magic phrase." "Start with this sentence." "Tell it to think step by step." Some of that stuff has its place — and we'll get into it later in this course. But none of it matters if the foundation isn't there. And the foundation is always context.

I've watched people spend twenty minutes engineering the perfect prompt structure when they could've spent thirty seconds just... explaining the situation. Just talking to it like a person. Telling it what's going on, what they need, and why.

That's the unlock. It's weirdly simple, and I think that's why people overlook it. They assume good AI outputs require sophisticated techniques. They don't. They require clear communication. And most of us are already good at clear communication — we just forget to do it when we're typing into a text box.

What Good Context Looks Like

When I say "give it context," I don't mean write an essay. I mean include the information that would matter to a smart person helping you with this task. Here's my general framework — and you don't need all of these every time, just the ones that are relevant:

Who is this for? Not just "a client" — which client? What's their role? What do they care about? What's your relationship with them?

What's the situation? What happened? What's the background? Why is this task happening right now?

What do you actually want? Not just the output format — the real goal. Are you trying to close a deal? Smooth over a conflict? Get someone excited about an idea?

What should it sound like? Your voice matters. Do you write casually or formally? Short sentences or long ones? Are you funny or straightforward? If you don't tell the AI, it's going to default to something that sounds like a LinkedIn post.

What should it NOT be? Sometimes the easiest way to guide the output is to say what you don't want. "Don't make it salesy." "Don't use bullet points." "Don't start with 'I hope this email finds you well.'"

That's it. That's the framework. Who, what's happening, what you want, how it should sound, and what to avoid. You don't need to memorize this. You just need to get in the habit of thinking about it for ten seconds before you type.

Try This Yourself

Here's something I want you to try right now, or the next time you're using Claude or any other AI tool.

Think of something you actually need to write today. An email, a message, a short document — something real. Don't make up a hypothetical.

First, type the prompt the way you normally would. Just your natural instinct. Send it and read what comes back.

Then, try again. But this time, before you type what you want, spend thirty seconds typing out the context. Who's involved, what the situation is, what tone you want, what you're trying to accomplish. Be specific. Use real names, real details, real constraints.

Compare the two outputs.

I'm willing to bet the second one is dramatically better. Not because you used some advanced technique. Because you communicated like a human being talking to another human being, instead of typing a keyword into a search bar.

Why It Works (The Deeper Layer)

There's a reason I'm starting the entire course with this. Everything else we're going to learn — how to iterate, how to manage context over long conversations, how to get AI to write in your voice, how to build systems and automations — all of it builds on this one idea.

If you internalize the habit of leading with context, you'll be better at using AI than ninety percent of people. Not because you know more prompting tricks. Because you understand what's actually happening in the conversation.

You're not giving commands to a machine. You're briefing a collaborator. And the better the briefing, the better the work.

I run my entire business with this mindset. When I need Claude to help me with a competitive analysis, I don't just say "analyze my competitors." I tell it about my company, my market, what I'm trying to figure out, and why it matters right now. When I need it to write ad copy, I don't say "write a Facebook ad." I describe the audience, the product, the emotional angle, the constraints, and what's worked before.

Every single interaction starts with: what does this tool need to know in order to help me well?

And then I just... tell it.

The Common Mistakes

Before we wrap up, let me hit the three biggest mistakes I see people make, now that you know what the shift is.

Mistake one: being vague because you think AI should "just know." It shouldn't. It can't. It's working with what you give it. If you give it nothing, it gives you nothing back. This isn't a flaw in the technology — it's the nature of how it works.

Mistake two: over-engineering the prompt structure instead of just providing information. I see people spending more time on prompt formatting than on actually explaining the task. They'll write "Act as a world-class marketing expert with 20 years of experience who has worked with Fortune 500 companies" and then give zero context about their actual situation. The role-playing can help in some cases, but it's never a substitute for real information.

Mistake three: giving up after one bad output. This is probably the biggest one. You try it once, get something generic, and conclude that AI can't do what you need. But you haven't actually tried. You've asked a vague question and gotten a vague answer. That's not failure — that's a starting point. Give it more context, tell it what was wrong, ask for a revision. The conversation is the product, not the first response.

What's Next

In the next lesson, we're going to break down the anatomy of a great prompt — the specific components you can include and how to structure them. But I want to be clear: structure is a tool, not a requirement. The most important thing you can do is what we talked about today. Lead with context. Communicate like a human. Treat the AI like a smart collaborator who needs to be briefed, not a search engine that needs a keyword.

Start there. Everything else gets easier.
